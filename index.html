The Manifesto

"A Written Testimony: MAKTUB is a sonic pilgrimage across the Atlantic — a dialogue between the descendants of the transatlantic divide, bound by shared struggle, divine purpose, and ancestral memory. Inspired by Minister Louis Farrakhan’s visits to South Africa in 1996 and 1998, the album imagines the ocean as both a wound and a bridge — carrying not just the ghosts of the past, but the echoes of resistance, faith, and rebirth. In 1996, Farrakhan’s meeting with President Nelson Mandela symbolized a convergence of two spiritual lineages of liberation: one born in Montgomery and Harlem, the other in Soweto and Robben Island. Mandela’s vision of non-racism, non-sexism, and religious tolerance reflected a new dawn; Farrakhan’s fire reminded the world that freedom without truth remains incomplete. Two years later, in 1998, when he returned to defend Winnie Madikizela-Mandela — calling her “the mother of a great struggle” — he affirmed the feminine divine within resistance, the matriarchal strength that sustains revolutions across oceans. Through this lens, MAKTUB becomes both testimony and prophecy. It is written — that Black struggle, whether in the streets of Chicago or the townships of Johannesburg, flows from one spiritual current. It is written — that the voices silenced by oppression will find rhythm in new tongues. And it is written — that even when separated by distance, the diaspora speaks one language: the language of faith, resistance, and transcendence. The album unfolds in three acts — The Descent, The Pilgrimage, and The Revelation — mirroring the journey from oppression to awakening. The production fuses ancestral drums and township choirs with boom-bap mysticism and cosmic jazz samples, blending the pulse of Johannesburg with the metaphysical soundscape of New Orleans. Jay Electronica’s verses serve as scripture — invoking prophets, ancestors, and revolutionaries — while the South African producer grounds it in the soil of a nation that once walked Mandela’s long road to freedom. By its end, MAKTUB is not merely an album; it’s a bridge — an offering to the ancestors who crossed the Atlantic in chains and those who broke them with song, prayer, and spirit."
